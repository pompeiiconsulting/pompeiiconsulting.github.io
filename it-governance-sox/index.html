Information Technology Governance

16 September 2016

IT Governance Structures

  • governance in the context of business enterprises is all about making decision that define expectation, grant authority, or ensure performance

  • IT governance is the operational process for how decisions will be made about the use of IT. It addresses decisions about resource allocation, project prioritization, performance measurements, cost determination, and value assessment of IT investments.

  • IT governance focusses on how decision rights can be distributed differently to facilitate centralize, decentralized, or hybrid modes of decision making

  • the organizational structure plays a major role

Centralized

  • bring together all staff, hardware, software, data, and processing into a single location

Centralized Advantages

  • Global standards and common data
  • “One voice” when negotiating supplier contracts
  • Greater leverage in deploying strategic IT initiatives
  • Economies of scale and a shared cost structure
  • Access to large capacity
  • Better recruitment and training of IT professionals
  • Better control of security and databases
  • Consistent with centralized enterprise structure

Centralized Disadvantages

  • Technology may not meet local needs
  • Slow support for strategic initiatives
  • Schism between business and IT organization
  • Us versus them mentality when technology problems occur
  • Lack of business unit control over overhead costs

Decentralized

  • the components in the centralized structure are scattered in different locations to address local business needs

Decentralized Advantages

  • Technology customized to local business needs
  • Closer partnership between IT and business units
  • Greater flexibility
  • Reduced telecommunication costs
  • Consistency with decentralized enterprise structure
  • Business unit control over overhead costs

Decentralized Disadvantages

  • Difficulty maintaining global standards and consistent data
  • Higher infrastructure costs
  • Difficulty negotiating preferential supplier agreements
  • Loss of control
  • Duplication of staff and data

Federalism

  • a combination of centralized and decentralized structures
  • Federalism is a structure that distributes power, hardware, software, data and personnel between a central IS group and IS in business units
  • It is considered a better IT governance structure for most cases
  • IT vision and leadership
  • group wide IT strategy and architecture
  • strategic control
  • synergy

Centralized

  • economies of scale
  • control of standards
  • critical mass of skills

Decentralized

  • users control IT priorities
  • business units have ownership
  • responsive to business unit needs

IT Governance

  • IT governance is also defined as specifying the decision rights and accountability framework to encourage desirable behavior in using IT
  • focus us not about what decisions are made but who makes decisions, who holds decision rights, and how the decision makers are held accountable
  • match the manager's decision rights with his or her accountability for a decision
  • mismatches result in an oversupply of IT resources or the inability of IT to meet business demand

High Accountability

High decision rights

  • strategic norm
  • level 3 balance
  • works where IT is viewed as component and strategic to business

Low decision rights

  • business gap
  • cost considerations dominate IT decisions
  • IT assets may not utilize internal competencies to meet business demand
  • IT group frustration with business group

Low Accountability

High decision rights

  • techno centric gap
  • danger of overspending on IT creating an oversupply
  • IT assets may not be utilized to meet business demand
  • business group frustration with IT group

Low decision rights

  • support norm
  • level 1 balance
  • works for organizations where IT is viewed as a support function
  • focus is on business efficiency

Information Technology Governance

  • good IT governance provides a structure to make good decisions
  • IT governance has two major components
    • the assignment of decision making authority and responsibility
    • the decision making mechanisms: steering committees, review boards, policies

  • Major categories of IT decisions

    • IT principles
    • IT architecture
    • IT infrastructure needs
    • IT investment and prioritization

  • IT Principles

    • high level statements about how IT is used in the business

  • IT Architecture

    • an integrated set of technical choices to guide the organization in satisfying business needs. The architecture is a set of policies and rules for the use of IT and plots a migration path to the way business will be done

  • IT Infrastructure Strategies

    • strategies for the base foundation of budgeted for IT capability both technical and human shared throughout the firm as reliable services and centrally coordinated

  • Business Application Needs

    • specification of the business need for purchased or internally developed IT applications

  • IT Investment and IT Prioritization

    • decision about how much and where to invest in IT including project approvals and justification techniques

Political Archetypes

  • archetype is a pattern for decision rights allocation and it labels the combination of people who provide information or have decision rights for key IT decisions
  • decisions can be made at three level
    • enterprise wide
    • business unit
    • region /group within a business unit
  • there are six IT governance archetypes
    • business monarchy,
    • IT monarchy
    • feudal
    • federal
    • duopoly
    • anarchy
  • duopoly is used by the largest portion of organizations for IT principle decisions

  • IT monarchy is the most popular for IT architecture and infrastructure decisions

  • Business Monarchy

    • a group of or individual business executive
    • includes committees comprised of senior business executives
    • excludes IT executives acting independently
    • may include CIO.

  • IT Monarchy

    • individuals or groups of IT executives

  • Feudal

    • business unit leaders key proves owners or their delegates

  • Federal

    • C level executives and at least one other business group
    • may include IT executives
    • equivalent to a country and its states working together

  • IT Duopoly

    • IT executives and one other croup

  • Anarchy

    • each individual user

IT Governance and IT Security

  • IT governance archetypes offers a new perspective for assigning responsibility for key security decisions
  • it identifies an appropriate governance pattern for each information security decision
  • information security strategy
    • business monarchy
  • information security policies
    • IT duopoly
  • information security infrastructure
    • IT monarchy
  • information security education/training
    • IT duopoly
  • information security investments
    • IT duopoly
  • the archetypes clearly define the responsibilities of the major players in the company

IT Decision Making Mechanisms

  • policies define the process of decision making in certain situations, but may be too rigid for a complex environment
  • 60% rely on policies making it the most popular IT governance mechanism
  • review board or committee formally designated to approve, monitor, and review specific topics can be an effective governance mechanism
  • IT steering committee is an advisory committee of key stakeholders or experts that provides guidance on important IT issues

Steering Committee

  • steering committee works well in the federal archetype which call for joint participation of IT and business leaders
  • IT governance council
    • steering committee at the highest level
    • reports to board or CEO
    • comprised of top-level executives
    • provides strategic direction and funding authority
  • lower level steering committees are responsible for effectively allocating scarce resources
  • companies usually have one or the other

Sarbanes-Oxley Act

  • the Sarbanes-Oxley (SoX) Act of 2002 was enacted to increase regulatory visibility and accountability of public companies and their financial health
  • all corporations under the SEC are subject to SOX requirements
    • US and foreign companies that are traded on US exchanges
    • companies that make up a significant part of a US companies financial reporting
  • CEOs and CFOs must personally certify and be accountable for their firm's financial records and accounting

COBIT

  • COBIT stands for Control Objectives for Information and Related Technology
  • IT governance framework for implementing SOX
  • focuses on making sure that IT provides the systematic rigor needed for SoX compliance
  • provides a framework for linking IT processes, IT resources, and IT information to a company's strategies and objectives
  • COBIT provides a set of process gals, metrics, and practices
  • risk categorization into four major domains
    • planning and organization
    • acquisition and implementation
    • delivery and support
    • monitoring
  • each company determines the processes that are the most susceptible to the risks that it chooses to manage
  • company identifies processes that it is going to manage
  • set up a control objective and specific key gal indicators
  • well suited to organizations focused on risk management and mitigation
  • designates clear ownership and responsibility for key processes in such a way that it is understood by all organizational stakeholders
  • COBIT provides a formal frame work for aligning IS strategy with the business strategy
  • very detailed
  • costly and time consuming
