Collaborative Business Processes

Pompeii Consulting | 07 Nov 2016

Due to deep changes in technology, demographics, business, the economy, and the world, we are entering a new age where people participate in the economy like never before. The new participation has reached a tipping point where new forms of mass collaboration are changing how goods and services are invented, produced, marketed and distributed on a global basis. The change presents far-reaching opportunities for every company and for every person who gets connected.

Most business professionals have heard of the new buzz words Web 2.0 and Enterprise 2.0. Initial definitions of these buzz words referred to new web technologies such as RSS feeds, wikis, blogs, social bookmarking, etc.) that became popular after the dot.com bust. These buzz words also indicate an increased intensity of collaboration and co-creation among peers that is revolutionizing the way companies and people interact.
We are all used to collaborating with others within our work environments think meetings, shared network drives, emails back and forth). We also collaborate outside of the workplace with people of similar interests home owners associations, church groups, volunteer organizations, parent-teacher associations). More recently, some of you may also have collaborated with people whom you have never met supply chain partners, various forums, Yahoo! or google groups, Facebook). These different spheres of collaborations Figure 1) basically happen inside organizations team collaborations), between organizations interG organizational collaborations), and within communities mass collaborations).

Team, Inter-organizational and mass collaborations are inherently different in purpose, context, and behavior. Traditionally, the boundaries between these three types of collaborations were clearly defined as well. However, new Web 2.0 technologies are blurring the boundaries between these different types of collaborations. Consider, for example, Research & Development – most companies would look to the internal R&D organization for ideas on new product developments and improvements to existing products. Today, many
firms examples include Procter & Gamble, 3M, NetFlix and GoldCorp) depend on open collaborative communities see for example, http://innocentive.com ) as well as internal R&D organizations to solve several vexing product development problems.
In this module, we will explore technology enabled collaboration models in each of the spheres of collaboration. We will discuss technologies, managerial issues and analysis techniques that help us better understand how effective collaborative models can be created and sustained.

Team Collaborations

Consider the following scenarios:

  • Imagine you have just won a project from a prestigious client. The client is a big pharmaceutical company with manufacturing facilities all around the globe. Your project is aimed at assessing the effectiveness of the client’s supply chain management practices in the Latin American manufacturing facilities. Although you have worked for over 10 years on various projects in the pharmaceutical industry, you have never headed a project in the Latin American countries before. You believe you need some members with previous Latin American project leadership experience on your team. You think Charles Turner, whom you had worked with two years ago on a project in Mexico, can help. You call Charles and explain your needs to him. While Charles would have liked to work with you, he is already committed for the next six months on other projects. However, Charles connects you with Mike Crain, who is a perfect match for your project. Charles sends an introductory email to Mike, and recommends that the three of you get together to negotiate an arrangement that meets your and Mike’s interests. At a subsequent meeting, not only does Mike agree to work on the project, he also brings with him three other experts who bring valuable pharmaceutical industry supply chain expertise to the project.

  • Your CEO calls you up late in the evening on Tuesday, informs you of a business opportunity, and asks you to prepare a proposal by Friday. Many of your trusted colleagues are spread around the world at this time. You know you can count on them to help, but you need some way to collaborate over email, IM, phone, or other means to prepare the proposal. Many of the members on your team, who will be needed to prepare the proposal, have not worked together before. You are worried that different versions of the proposal will be flying around over the emails that will make it hard for the team to collaborate. You also wonder whether they will have the right chemistry to handle such a high pressure deadline in a matter of four days.

Situations like these are quite common in today’s global corporations. GeographicallyG dispersed teams are often formed in a matter of days; then the teams have to quickly learn how to collaborate and contribute effectively towards the success of the project. While traditionally members from a single organization would collaborate, today it is quite common to see teams from different companies come together to design, develop, or market a product or service. A more recent trend is also to have an “open source” approach, where any member of the public can potentially contribute to the development of a product or idea.

In these types of processes, collaboration, information exchange, and work culture play a crucial role. The effectiveness of the process is dependent upon how well the team members connect with each other and how the culture of collaboration is intrinsically absorbed by all those who participate.

The modeling approach outlined in the previous module has only a limited role to play in these types of contexts. Therefore, we will explore emerging issues with respect to how organizations are approaching the design of collaborative business processes and discuss how existing business models are being radically changed as a result of the advances in collaboration technologies.

Classifying Collaborative Work

Researchers have attempted to classify collaborative tasks along many dimensions. The main intent behind these classifications is to better understand how collaborative work can be supported with technology.
One way to classify collaborative tasks is based on time and space dimensions1. On the time dimension, collaborations in teams may happen synchronously e.g., a face-to-face conversation) or collaboration may happen asynchronously e.g., by email). On the space dimension, all collaborators may be in the same location e.g., in a meeting room) or at different locations e.g., via a conference call or webinar).

It is also important to consider what types of tasks are accomplished in teams. McGrath 1984)2 classified group tasks into four types in a framework called task!circumplex: Generative, Choice, Negotiation, and Execution see Figure 3).

  • Generative tasks are creative tasks, including those that generate new ideas or new plans.
  • Choice tasks include problem solving tasks where there is a correct answer; and decision-making tasks where a preferred answer is chosen.
  • Negotiation tasks are characterized by resolution of conflicts – for example, policy formulations. ￼2 McGrath, J. E. 1984). Groups: Interaction and performance. Englewood Cliffs, NJ: Prentice Hall.￼
  • Execution tasks that may be primarily physical for example, construction jobs) and those that are measured against some objective standards most multi-step workflow processes).

Using this framework, it is also possible to see how interdependence between team members manifests. For instance, Generation tasks benefit from close cooperation between team members. Choice and Execute tasks benefit from increased levels of coordination between team members. Negotiation to an extent, Execution and Choice) tasks require considerations for resolving conflicts.
You will readily notice that we can place collaboration tools and technologies in the Time Space dimension as in Figure 4. Notice that some of the technologies under different time/different place can also be used in the other quadrants. However, as we begin to focus more on the types of tasks performed by groups, the question of task-technology fit becomes more salient. For example, what types of collaboration technologies are good for coordination intensive tasks? Are there collaboration technologies that help in generation or negotiation tasks? These are harder questions to answer.
In many cases, the organizational context and culture further complicate the use of collaboration technologies. Verbal and non-verbal cues in collaborative activities are extremely important for the team members as they convey valuable information and feedback in group activities. Collaboration technologies are inherently imperfect in conveying these visual cues. Additionally, collaboration technologies can change the way group members interact. For example, multiple members can simultaneously type their views in a chat room session; however, in a face-to-face meeting, it is difficult for team members to simultaneously speak. However, the multiple messages appearing in the chat room session can make it difficult for users to follow the discussions.
We will explore the task-technology fit in a bit more detail in the following section.

Fit between Collaboration Technologies and Collaboration Tasks

While the time-space dimension is a useful way to understand different types of collaboration needs, the most common basis on which collaboration technologies are classified is based on the type of task support they provide. Collaboration technologies and systems provide three basic types of task support:

Communication Support

Communication aspects of collaboration systems support, define, and enhance the ability of group members to exchange information. Communication capabilities may include group displays, simultaneous input, and anonymous communications.

Process Structuring

Collaboration system-specific processes define the way groups interact in group tasks. These capabilities include creation and enforcement of team agendas.

Information Processing

Collaboration systems can structure, aggregate, and analyze the information exchanged between team members.

Collaboration system features may cover some or all of these features – although, typically they focus more deeply on one of the three aspects. For example, workflow systems provide process structuring support; blogs provide communication support; and, group voting systems provide information processing support.

Collaboration Systems Usage in Organizations

IBM, with over 300,000 employees worldwide, was one of the pioneers of collaboration systems usage. IBM used several different tools to enhance knowledge and information sharing, and collaborations across time and geographic boundaries. A tool called, TeamRooms a Lotus Notes-based collaboration tool), was to enable employees to setup shared collaborative spaces. Eventually, over 27,000 TeamRooms were active within IBM5. IBM also operated periodic focused collaborative sessions on various topics. A recent collaboration forum called Global InnovationJam was run over multiple days that had over 50,000 participants from around the world. These participants included IBM employees and their family members in addition to IBM’s customers and business partners.

Today, organizations can choose collaboration tools and technologies from multiple vendors. Capabilities range from generic Enterprise level collaboration tools to process-specific implementation of collaboration capabilities within industries and organizations. The wide variety of tools does not actually reduce the risks of choosing the wrong strategy for collaborative processes. In my opinion, it may distract managers from the real issues at hand for designing effective collaborative structures within teams.

The years of experience with collaboration technologies within IBM has highlighted issues related to adoption and effectiveness of these technologies6. Most importantly, collaboration success depends on how well the team members adapt to a new way of working and not on technology itself. Since our discussions so far have stressed the need to understand the context of collaboration, it is instructive to review the types of teams we encounter in organizations. Team leaders who understand the team context are better equipped to deal with them. Sundstrom et al 1990) provide a typology of work teams which helps illustrate four major types of teams7.

Advice teams

like committees, review panels, and advisory councils, are created to broaden the information base for managerial decision-making. Advice teams require a low degree of technical specialization and coordination and, depending on their purpose, can have short or long life spans. Some of their typical outputs are decisions, suggestions, and recommendations. These types of teams rarely need specialized collaboration systems, unless the committee draws its membership from across different geographical or organizational boundaries.

Production teams

like assembly teams, data processing groups, and maintenance crews, are responsible for performing day-to-day operations. While the minimal training required for routine tasks calls for a low level of technical specialization, these teams do require a high degree of coordination. Some typical outputs for these teams include components, assemblies, and customer service. These types of teams benefit the most from coordination support through workflow management and other similar applications.
￼￼

Project teams

like research groups, planning teams, engineering teams, and task forces, require creative problem solving involving high degrees of specialized knowledge. Coordination for these teams can range from low traditional units) to high crossGfunction units). Examples of outputs are plans, designs, prototypes, and reports of findings. These types of teams benefit from communication and coordination capabilities in the collaborative system.

Action teams

like sports teams, negotiating teams, military squads, and entertainment groups, involve high degrees of both technical specialization and coordination. In other words, these are examples of brief performance events often repeated under new conditions. Typical examples include combat missions, expeditions, and competitive events. These types of teams benefit from collaboration systems that emphasize coordination and information processing capabilities.

It is also possible that teams can evolve from one type to another or can have components of all four types. For example, project team composition and responsibilities can change throughout the lifecycle of the project.

Modeling Techniques for Collaborative Processes

Several useful modeling approaches can be beneficial in collaborative process contexts. In the following, two important modeling approaches are described. The first, Design Structure Matrix, helps in appropriately designing a collaborative process based on the information dependencies between the various sub-processes. The second, Social Network Analysis, helps in addressing the deficiencies and strengths of a team’s social structure.

Design Structure Matrix (DSM)

In iterative business processes, it is important to consider the sequencing of processes since the processes can be highly interdependent. Note that the dependency here need not be from a workflow perspective. For example, when one is designing a new product, the different components in the product can be independently developed as long as the groups share the design information with each other. However, if one group has to change the design specifications, it can have cascading effects on rest of the product teams. Therefore, sequencing the development of the different components based on dependencies can create efficiencies in product development process. Similarly, in software development projects it is important to sequence the development of software components such that the dependencies are optimally accounted for and there is minimization of rework when software specifications are altered during the development process.

Design Structure Matrix is a modeling approach that assists in optimally designing an iterative process using dependency relationships between various sub-processes or components. The dependencies are depicted in the matrix in different ways. One approach is to simply represent whether any two components have dependencies in binary format e.g., ‘1’ indicating dependency, and ‘0’ indicating no dependency; alternatively, ‘x’ indicating dependency, and a blank indicating no dependency). It is also possible to show strength of dependency between components by using scaled entries e.g., dependency strengths could be shown on a scale of 1 to 10 with higher numbers indicating stronger dependency).

DSM is a square matrix i.e., same number of rows and columns) and the columns and rows represent the components or sub-processes. Forward dependency is represented in entries below!the!diagonal, and iteration or feedback loop) is represented in entries above!the!diagonal. We will use an example see Table 2) related to Kodak’s design process adapted from Ulrich and Eppinger 1999) to illustrate the modeling concept8. The different sub-processes here are typical of a product design process. When reading a row, a ‘1’ in a cell Row: I, Col: J), indicates that the sub-process represented in row I receives input from the sub-process in Column J. For example, activity, 3:Design Beta Widgets, is dependent on or receives input from) activities 1, 2 and 6. Reading Column 3 for the same activity indicates that it provides input to Activities 4, 5, 6 and 7.
￼￼ The entries above the diagonal in the matrix marked in red) indicate feedback loops or iterations. Iterations in iterative processes create rework and can delay the process cycle time. For example, any rework due to feedback from “Test beta Widgets” to “Design Beta Widgets” can cascade into additional work for sub-processes that are dependent on “Design Beta Widgets.” Thus, it is important to a) minimize feedback loops, and b) minimize the consequences of feedback loops on the process. When the above-diagonal entries are close to the diagonal, the impact of feedback loops are minimized. It is also possible to combine subG processes i.e., reorganize the teams for better collaboration) and reduce the impact of rework.

We will briefly review the concepts related to social network analysis next.

Social Network Analysis (SNA)

A social network is a representation of relationships between people or organizations. Social networks can be shown either in the form of a network diagram or in a matrix form. While the SNA method has been used in academic disciplines for many years, improved computing power has enabled the modeling of large scale networks in recent years. SNA is a popular analytic technique in public health, management, anthropology, biology and other fields.

Social networks are built in a group context where information on relationships is obtained by surveying the group members or by observing the communications patterns of the group members emails, meetings, previous engagements, etc.).

To illustrate, let’s consider a group consisting of five members: Adam, Bryan, Cindy, David, and Erin. The relationship between these members is shown in the form of a network and a matrix in Figure 5 and Table 2.
In the matrix Table 4) we represent the existence of a relationship between two individuals by placing a 1 in the corresponding row and column. If no relationship exists, a 0 is entered. It is straightforward to move from the matrix form of the social network to the network form. We represent each individual as a node in the network. A link is drawn between two nodes if a 1 exists in the cell corresponding to the two nodes in the matrix.

￼￼￼￼The network shown in Figure 5 is bi-directional, i.e., there is symmetry in the relationship between any two nodes. Social relationships need not always be symmetrical. For example, when communication patterns are used to construct relationships, it might be possible that the communication always happens in one direction say, Adam writes to Bryan, but Bryan never writes to Adam). Similarly, if we are capturing information through a survey and ask the respondents to indicate who they would approach for advice, the resulting network is also not likely to have symmetry.

Social Network Metrics

When large numbers of individuals are included in the network, the visualization of the network structure may provide valuable insights. However, for a more thorough analysis, different types of metrics may need to be computed. The following is a sample list of such social network metrics.
Degree'Centrality: Degree centrality simply measures the number of connections an individual has with others in the network. Nodes with more connections have more options for their communications needs. Degree centrality is often a measure of power and prestige within the network.
In Figure 5, Adam has a degree centrality of 2; Bryan has a degree centrality of 3.

Another approach to defining centrality in a network is to use the closeness!centrality measure. One way to define closeness centrality is to find the number of connections that an individual must go through to reach everyone else in the network.
For each member represented Figure 5, the network requires at most two connections to reach everyone else in the network.

When you order the nodes in the network by the centrality measure, you will be able to identify individuals or entities that are central to the relationships and communications within the group.

Network Density

Network density is the proportion of connections in a network relative to the total number of connections possible i.e., if every node is connected to every other node). Density is a measure of how closely knit the group is. If the group has a high number of connections, the group is likely to be more closely knit and collaborative.

Group Cohesion

Cohesion in a group implies that there are multiple connections and paths between actors. In a sense, network density is one measure of cohesion. When multiple paths exists between nodes, the group is more resilient to loss of a node for example, a team member quits the firm) and communication between nodes is faster. Cohesion is also tied to higher levels of trust and collaboration.
Group cohesion apart from network density) is measured using the average of the number of connections for one node to reach another node in the network. The average over the entire network gives a measure of the level of cohesion in the network. Group cohesion can be applied to subsets of nodes within the network as well to analyze the cohesiveness of cliques within the teams).

Core periphery and Clique Structures

Visualizing the network structures provides valuable insights on the nature of cohesion and subgroups in teams. Two popular ways of characterizing overall network structures are the corePperiphery and clique structures.

In a core-periphery structure, there is a set of nodes that are central to the network i.e., a larger number of connections) and a number of other nodes exist at the periphery of the network i.e., fewer connections). The central nodes are the information brokers i.e., communicate information to others).
In a clique structure, a single core does not exist. Instead, a number of closely knit subP groups connect to each other and the sub-groups connect to other sub-groups through a smaller number of connections.
Figure 7 illustrates social networks with core-periphery and clique structures.

￼Core-periphery structures have been noticed in some firms where the nodes in the periphery were more likely to be top managers and the nodes in the core tended to be middle level managers. This is not necessarily a good scenario as the senior managers may be out of sync with their own organization. In such situations, interventions may be necessary to introduce the top managers back into the core of the network.

Clique structures are more common in firms that have recently acquired new firms. The informal networks of the old firms have been known to persist even in the new organization despite changes to the formal organization structure. Again, specific interventions may be necessary to break the cliques.

Collecting Network Data

Interestingly, basic information required to build social networks already exist in firms. To build networks for SNA one can use the following sources of information:

  • Email archives
  • Conference call records and meeting minutes
  • Documents produced in recent history by different teams in the firm
  • Expertise profile and previous assignments

Analysts can use these sources of information to get initial insights on the type of networks that exist within the firm. However, relationships are contextual in nature – we approach different people for different needs. Therefore, employee surveys may be necessary to gain insights on specific types of informal networks. Past research on SNA, especially in corporate contexts, indicates that different types of informal networks may provide valuable information to analysts and managers9,10:

Information Networks

Individuals seek out and approach their colleagues for advice and information. Survey questions can be designed to specifically seek out these types of information-based relationships. Information networks reveal how well employees know each other’s knowledge, skills, and abilities.
Access'Networks:'Even when we know someone has the necessary skills or knowledge to address the problems we face, they may not be readily accessible. Accessibility may be hampered by geographic distance, organizational structure, or interpersonal relationships. Access-related survey questions could probe accessibility of individuals within the information network from the perspectives of timely availability, quality of interaction, and engagement.

Trust Networks

When faced with really complex and sensitive issues, employees tend to approach only those they fully trust for advice and problem-solving help. In essence, this would be a network that the employee engages with in crisis situations. Naturally, Trust networks tend to be smaller than Access networks.

Clique structures are also more likely to show up in Trust and Access networks. Information networks may be more likely to exhibit core-periphery structure.

You may have already noticed that the type of information gathered in building social networks is quite sensitive. It is usually advisable to engage an outside consulting firm to explore the social networks with the assurance that collected data will not be shared with others including senior management) in detailed format. The discussions and deliberations should be based on the overall network structure with identifying information removed) and not on individual relationships.

Technology and Organizational Interventions

Specific interventions to improve social relationships can be undertaken based on SNA results. Technology and organizational interventions can be designed to improve the network structure.

Clique Structure

In cross-functional organizations, such as consulting firms, employees bring different skill types and orientations. Social relations in these firms can be fragmented leading to sub-groups within each functional area technology experts, strategy experts, marketing experts, etc.). Organizational measures that bring together these disparate groups could include formation of cross-functional teams and mixed incentive structures to promote collaboration. Increasing face-to- face interactions among the group members would have more impact before any collaboration technology solution is considered.

Bottlenecks

While core-periphery structure in a social network is desirable in organizational contexts, it can lead to a few individuals becoming bottlenecks. Information flow in the network can become too dependent on one or two individuals. These individuals can become bogged down through their involvement in multiple teams and meetings thus becoming a bottleneck for all collaborative work. When such situations are encountered, specific managerial interventions will be needed to reduce the load on the central individual s) to identify other employees who can share the load in the social network.

Sparse Connections

Fewer connections i.e., low density) in the network are not conducive to smooth coordination in teams. To alleviate this problem, a firm could consider technology solutions such as Corporate Yellow Pages to improve the information network) and corporate forums to improve the access network). These technology solutions can be augmented with managerial interventions such as explicit incentives for participating in corporate forums and experts networks, training and education to increase interpersonal trust, and corporate fairs and symposia to foster new network connections.

Low levels of Access

Sparsely connected access networks may indicate a lack of communication and information processing abilities in the networks. If employees rely on synchronous media such as phone and conference calls) for access, it might considerably delay the ability of people to communicate with each other. If synchronous media is augmented with asynchronous collaboration technologies such as workflow systems, blogs and wikis, new channels of communication can improve the access network.
SNA can be especially useful in selecting influential executives for high-level positions, identification of task group members for major organizational restructuring efforts, and postP merger activities.

Inter-organizational collaborations

Traditionally, inter-organizational relationships were viewed from a “make or buy” perspective. This transactional perspective envisioned an arms-length relationship with partner organizations. Such a narrow view to inter-organizational relationships is no longer valid. Today’s firms collaborate with partner firms at strategic, tactical, and transactional levels. In fact, the term Organizational,Eco/systems is increasingly being used to indicate closely interconnected community of organizations that collaborate through long-term relationships. These new forms of collaborations can happen through several different arrangements – strategic partnerships and alliances; long term supply contracts; outsourcing contracts; joint investments and ventures; marketing alliances; etc. These collaborative partnerships are intended to reduce risks, better utilize complementary assets, access new markets, and quicken the pace of industry innovations. Given the use of complex technologies in today’s products and services, no single firm may have the necessary technology or skill sets to introduce new products and services at the pace required to maintain its competitive advantage. In this context, inter-organizational collaborations are emerging as an attractive alternative to pool the resources of multiple organizations.

Collaboration Models

Key Enablers of Collaboration

Even when inter-organizational collaborations are conceived at the strategic level, execution of the strategy determines the payoffs from the relationship. Toyota Corp. is one of the pioneering companies to create knowledge-sharing networks in the auto industry. Toyota’s collaboration processes with partner organizations have been extensively studied by industry analysts and academicians to understand the success of their collaboration model. The key enablers of effective collaboration in the organizational ecosystems include:

Shared Purpose

The partners in the ecosystems should have the same sense of purpose to openly share knowledge with other firms. This may require some of the larger organizations within the network to subsidize initial participation to alleviate concerns of the smaller partners about the utility of sharing their proprietary knowledge within the network.
Shared purpose in the Toyota network is created through:
• The creation of a Supplier Association that acts as a community forum for social interactions. • A network-level consulting division with the objective of fostering learning and knowledge sharing among the partner firms. • Smaller voluntary groups of suppliers that share a closer set of objectives to improve productivity and address quality problems. • Job rotations for employees of partner firms at the network level.

Rules and Norms for Participation

One of the major problems in teams of individuals or firms is that many may tend to “freePride” on other contributors. Collaboration networks need to avoid this trap by creating rules and norms that protect the members against free-riders. The network should not hesitate to remove partner organizations that violate the norms of participation.
Rules and norms in the Toyota network include:
• Production processes within the Toyota network is not considered proprietary knowledge. All members have open access to production-related processes and technologies. • Only key product design details are considered proprietary knowledge of individual firms. • Any value appropriated from knowledge transfer resides with the recipient for at least 1P2 years, with the expectation that the network partners will share the benefits after the initial period.
3. Cohesive-and Dense Network: It is important to create a network structure that is resilient in information transfer and collaboration. This means that multiple pathways should exist between partner organizations. The redundancy of paths helps in rapid transfer of knowledge.
At Toyota, the networks established at multiple levels supplier association, smaller voluntary groups, and network level consulting division) facilitate the creation of a cohesive and dense social network within the ecosystem. The different types of activities within these networks facilitate sharing of both tacit and explicit knowledge.

Role of Technology in Inter-organizational Collaboration

Technology can play a facilitating role in inter-organizational collaboration. At the same time, technology implementation and usage in inter-organizational networks is fraught with pitfalls and risks due to disparate technology platforms and organizational structures.

Depending on the type of collaboration process, collaborative systems can be used extensively and integrated deeply into the business process or collaborative systems can be used in a facilitating role for the main business process. Design processes benefit from deep penetration of collaborative IT systems as these processes are iterative and involve highly frequent inter-personal collaborations. While supply chain, manufacturing, and logistics processes require close integration of transactional systems, they may also benefit from collaborative capabilities that provide information processing and coordination support. These capabilities enhance the effectiveness of the process integration at the operational levels between firms.

Boeing’s use of collaborative technologies to bring together partner firms in its product design and production efforts is one illustration of the successful use of IT in collaborative ecoP systems14. Boeing uses Catia V5, CAD software from Dassault systems of Germany, to connect hundreds of partner organizations. Boeing requires that all partner firms use the same software and the same version for easy exchange of information. Boeing also requires that every person from any firm) involved in the design effort receive software updates at the same time. These updates involve over 100 applications used in the design process. A data warehouse for storing design and engineering information is also created. Partner firms upload their design changes on a periodic basis so that the latest designs are available to the entire team.

The standardized Collaborative,Planning,,Forecasting,and,Replenishment CPFR) process in retail supply chains is an instance illustrating how IT support for information processing is helpful. Inventory management in the retail industry’s supply chains is a fairly complex process. The uncertainties in customer demand faced by downstream partners get amplified as the information is processed upstream in the supply chain. This causes each upstream partner to carry higher levels of inventory i.e., “safety stock”). Clearly, this is insufficient as the costs of carrying excess inventory is eventually incurred by everyone involved. To help alleviate the forecasting and planning problems in retail supply chains, the CPFR process lays out a standard set of activities for supply chain collaborators more details on the CPFR process is available at: http://www.vics.org/committees/cpfr/ ). Managers in the supply chain have to continuously collaborate and exchange information on plans, forecasts, store promotions, orders, lead times, and deliveries. Typically, a retail store would have hundreds of store locations, carry several hundred SKU stock carrying units), and manage several distribution centers and warehouses.

Collaborative systems in such situations provide information processing support. For example, an exception event a new promotion) can cause a change in the order placement plans of the retailer. In these cases, collaboration systems can help supply chain partners by alerting them to misalignment in plans and forecasts. The exception alerts can focus the collaborative efforts on the exception events and help reduce the information burden on the managers.

The process change pitfalls we discussed in the first module apply in the implementation of collaboration systems as well. In an inter-organizational context, the pitfall risks are elevated due to the confluence of multiple organizations in the collaboration network. These pitfalls are listed below.

• Resistance – Disagreements over the process change decisions and/or process design that cause the process change effort to stall. • Inertia – Lack of progress on the process change effort despite supposed) agreement on the process design details. • Misspecification – Making incorrect process design decisions, creating a process or technology that “works” but is a misfit. Once a new process design is rolled out, there is a danger that we encounter two additional pitfalls:
• Misuse – Improper employment of a new technology/process; in a sense, sabotaging the process design through creation of informal and undocumented processes and workarounds. • Nonuse'– Lack of adoption of the process or technology; this behavior especially manifests when employees perceive the process or technology as discretionary. The following key points should be considered and agreed upon when implementing collaborative technologies in inter-organizational contexts:
• Is their prior agreement on the use of the system among partner organizations? • Do all members have access to the same version of the technology platform? • Who is responsible for providing training and support? • How are the costs of implementing and maintaining the system shared across partner organizations? • If disputes arise in the use and performance of the system, what is the process used for resolving the disputes? • Who retains data ownership in the collaboration system? If it is shared, what are the standards for use and dissemination of information? • If the system can have widespread use in the industry, can the process and technology features be adopted as an industry standard much like CPFR)?

Examples of Innovative Collaboration Models

Collaborations between firms need not always be based on technology integration or technology usage. A few such examples of innovative collaborations are discussed below.
Netbank was an Internet-only bank that had no physical branches unfortunately, this bank went bankrupt in 2007 in the wake of the mortgage crisis)15. Most of its retail customers had to use pre-paid envelopes to deposit checks. The cycle time to complete the deposit transactions used to be around 7 days. The process was also expensive for NetBank as it paid for postage and envelope costs. While using ATMs from other banks was an option, it would have been cost0-prohibitive for NetBank to refund transaction charges to its customers for deposits. NetBank also considered the other costly option of investing in a network of ATM machines. In looking for ideal locations to put the teller machines, it began talking to UPS. UPS had a network of over 3500 retail stores across America. However, it was not interested in hosting ATMs in its retail stores. Eventually, the two firms came up with an interesting solution: Bank customers could go to any UPS store and drop their deposit envelopes and obtain a receipt. The UPS store, at the end of the day, would consolidate all the envelopes and overnight it to NetBank’s processing center. The arrangement benefited both UPS and NetBank. NetBank could process deposits in two days instead of the normal seven days. UPS would also benefit from the increased foot-traffic to its retail store. This arrangement needed no IT integration or collaboration technologies!

The Chinese motorcycle industry is an example of successful modularization of product development and production16. The Chinese motorcycle industry is concentrated in the city of Chongqing. The industry consists of several hundred loosely coupled firms that coordinate to design and produce motorcycles and motorcycle components. The firms involved in the network collaboratively modify the specifications to make rapid improvements in product design. Since the firms are co-located in the same city, there are ample opportunities for faceP to-face interactions for testing out the design ideas. These loosely-coupled collaborations in the industry have been spectacularly successful. Chinese-manufactured motorcycle sales have grown from 5 million in 1997 to over 21 million in 2006.
As technology takes center stage with the widespread acceptance of e-business, it is increasingly the case that IT standards become both a facilitator and, in some cases, the very subject of e-business. In today’s business environment the need for developing widelyP accepted rules of engagement or standards for technology is principally to facilitate interoperability among information products and services. However, standards-setting processes involve interactions between firms with often conflicting objectives. In addition, standards-setting processes are massive collaboration efforts – in all over 520 organizations have been identified by XML.ORG, an independent industry group that keeps track of ongoing XML standardization efforts. As active players attempt to influence XML-based eP business standards for specific industries and in the cross industry contexts, the group of firms engaged in creating and promoting standards use a variety of media to collaborate and resolve conflicts. Collaboration processes include participation in industry consortia, face-to-face meetings and teleconferences, and online discussion groups. A close examination of the social networks formed in the standards bodies reveals a robust community of subject matter experts who have built lasting social networks. These experts often share a common vision for the evolution of the e-business industry and associated technologies.

Sustaining and Growing Peer Communities

In the ’90s, Internet usage grew rapidly as the number of servers and clients connected to it exponentially increased. Interestingly, none of the growth was really planned – we “selfP organized” into a massive community for information-sharing within a short amount of time. In a similar way, peer communities for mass collaborations are “self-organizing.” This would imply that mass collaboration is inherently a messy and random process. This indeed was the initial thought-process related to Internet growth.
More recently, however, we are beginning to unearth patterns in how the Internet has inherently self-organized in a systematic fashion at least from the perspective of the connections between web pages). Among the earliest works, Barabasi and Albert 1999) showed that the pattern of connectivity between pages on the WWW follows a power-law distribution17. If one were to go with the initial assumption that the pages are connected to each other in a random fashion, then the distribution of the number of connections per page should be even i.e., each page is equally likely to be connected to any other page). However, what really emerged is that a very small percentage of the pages had a very large number of connections and a very large percentage of the pages had very few connections. This follows what is called a power law distribution see Figure 8).

What this implies is that as the WWW grew, the new pages were likely to be designed in such a way that they linked to pages which already had a large number of connections. Interestingly, this is how many social networks behave. People desire to create connections with those who already are well-connected. A side benefit is that the number of links that we must go through to reach a random node can be reduced considerably. This is why human networks are termed “small-world networks.” It takes an incredibly small number of links to connect one person to any random person in the country!

￼￼ Many social networking sites try to take advantage of this phenomenon. For example, LinkedIn can build a remarkably close-knit community through their social networking model. The effectiveness of the network grows as the number of people who signPin grows as well.

So, how does the “small-world network” connect to mass collaboration communities? It turns out that even content contributions and subscriptions follow the “small-world” pattern. For example, the contribution to readership ratio on Wikipedia is very low, i.e., a very small percentage of users contribute and a very large percentage of readers benefit! According to Wikipedia founder, Jimmy Wales, 10 percent of all users make 80 of all edits; 5 percent of all users make 66 of edits; half of all edits are made by just 2 1⁄2 of all users. So, power-law distribution still rules.

A low threshold level of contribution creates additional incentives for participants. For example, in open source software development communities, users can contribute by either submitting code changes or by contributing bug reports. A very small percentage of users contribute to code changes because of the high threshold level aka scale-free pattern). However, a larger percentage of users report bugs because of the low threshold level for contribution.
Similarly, on social bookmarking sites, there is a low threshold level for participation – you simply need to contribute a bookmark. On Wikipedia, there is a lower level threshold for doing minor edits for example, correcting spelling mistakes, adding a sentence or two to complete the article). Therefore, a larger number of contributors are seen to play the role of “editors” and “synthesizers,” a smaller number of contributors actually need to create “new” articles.

So, for a collaborative community to be successful, it is important to have a large user base. Even if a small number of these users are contributors, the community can be sustainable. The long tail in the power law distribution is the “lurker” population – but it is an integral part of the community as it keeps the community vibrant. Without this community, the small set of contributors could get discouraged. If possible, a low threshold level for participation is desirable. If not, the community should be set up to allow for multiple levels of participation so that contributors can self-organize at the levels they are willing to participate.

Crowd sourcing

The Open Source Software OSS) community is a peculiar phenomenon. It is a seemingly unrelated set of people who may have never met, gathering in digital space to build fairly complex software products including operating systems, browsers, and server applications). Commercially-equivalent products of this nature are built by corporations that employ hundreds of highly-paid programmers and systems architects and spend millions of dollars in design and development.
While the open source movement creates a number of interesting societal questions on intellectual property rights, it also brings forth the vast potential and abilities of self-organized, openPended communities to carry out intellectually-complex projects. Many firms have begun to utilize open source communities and even encourage their employees to contribute and participate in creating new products. Firms like IBM and Redhat generate revenue from providing complementary services and support for open source products. Interestingly, the open source model is being extended in unexpected ways by some firms in other industries. Jeff Howe http://crowdsourcing.com) defines crowd sourcing as “nthe act of taking a job traditionally performed by a designated agent usually an employee) and outsourcing it to an undefined, generally large group of people in the form of an open call.n Or “the application of Open Source principles to fields outside of software.” A sampling of crowd sourcing approaches used by NetFlix, Innocentive.com, Rentacoder.com, and GoldCorp are an indicator of the future of collaboration in corporate settings.
• NetFlix, an online, DVD rental company that has challenged the statusPquo of the storePbased DVD rental business dominated by Blockbuster. At the heart of NetFlix’s business strategy is its ability to predict whether a customer would like or not like a movie. The Cinematch recommendation system that NetFlix uses mines millions of movie rentals to come up with accurate predictions. Obviously, NetFlix wants these predictions to be as accurate as possible. While it continues to work on improving its prediction algorithm, it also threw a challenge in 2006 to the outside world to improve upon the prediction accuracy. The “NetFlix Prize” http://www.netflixprize.com/index) promised to award $1 Million to anyone who improved the prediction accuracy by at least 10 over Cinematch’s current capabilities. It provided a training dataset of actual movie rentals from its data warehouse for contestants to test their algorithm. At the beginning of 2008, there were 30,056 registered contestants on 24,000 teams from 167 different countries. Over 20,000 valid submissions were received from 2876 different teams. These teams were been able to improve upon prediction accuracy by over 8 as against the goal for grand prize – 10 ). NetFlix also awarded $50,000 for a team that was first to reach a target rate of accuracy set for the year. The winning team for the $1 million prize was a group of employees from AT&T Research Labs! • Innocentive.com is an open innovation community that won the Economist magazine’s “Business Process Award.” Innocentive.com has created a marketplace for firms to expand their R&D capabilities by reaching out to a vast network of researchers and experts all around the world. As an intermediary, Innocentive.com manages the intellectual property transfer issues for the solvers and seeking firms. Firms that announce a challenge associate an award amount for the solver. The firm can select from a set of solutions provided by the solvers that best meets its requirements. Interestingly, the firm does not have to pay for failed attempts at solutions. Over 125,000 solvers are registered on Innocentive.com from highly scientific and specialized fields such as chemistry, life sciences, and physical sciences. Several major companies including Boeing, Dow Chemical, Eli Lilly and Company, and Procter & Gamble post challenges regularly on Innocentive.com.
• Rentacoder.com is the ebay equivalent of software programming services. Rentacoder.com allows buyers to post programming problems or systems development project requests online. Nearly 200,000 registered programmers on the site can bid for these projects. As in ebay, programmers and buyers build reputation scores over time. Buyers can place their requests for free and once they accept a bid can place the negotiated amount in an escrow account. The programmer will get paid once they complete the systems development. Most of the requests are for projects that would be too small to be contracted to outsourcing companies. Many of the programmers are from low-cost countries. To maintain a sustainable community of programmers and buyers, Rentacoder.com publishes top programmer lists and Expert Guarantee features aka a certification program).
Rentacoder.com is not without competition. Topcoder.com is another firm that uses the crowd sourcing strategy with a different twist. Topcoder.com uses weekly competitions to rate its base of thousands of software developers around the world. Only those who qualify are eligible for working on projects. Topcoder.com uses a specialized methodology to build software by drawing from the army of qualified developers around the world. Each project is divided into smaller parts and opened up as a competition for its developer community. The best solution is selected from the pool of solutions provided by the developers. Topcoder.com then assembles the final project by integrating the different components together.
• GoldCorp is a Canadian Gold Mining Corporation. Goldcorp in 1999 faced a tough problem – its 50PyearPold mine in Red Lake, Ontario was facing high costs and depleting gold deposits. While some test drilling at remote sites revealed possibly rich deposits of gold, the firm’s geologists were unable to accurately estimate the amount of gold deposits and the exact locations. The CEO of GoldCorp CEO at that time, Rob McEwen, surprised everyone by releasing the geological data to the entire world under the “GoldCorp Challenge” http://www.goldcorpchallenge.com/). Several submissions came in from around the world that identified over a 100 areas for possible exploration. A substantial number of the identified areas yielded gold. Between 2000 and 2001, gold production increased by nearly 500 in the Red Lake area. The top four finalists in the competition were awarded $325,000.
While these success stories point to the advantages of crowd sourcing, there are also troubling issues that managers have to be aware of when embarking on this business model. First, there is an element of exploitation that one has to carefully address. The competitions may appear to exploit individuals’ time and effort, especially when the competition reward and resulting benefits to the firm are not well-matched. Second, as more firms follow the crowd sourcing model, the novelty of the approach may wear out and thus may possibly diminish the quality of the final solutions. Finally, when customer data is released, firms may inadvertently breach privacy rules and standards. For example, when AOL released its customer search queries database, it inadvertently revealed embarrassing details about individual customers not surprisingly, search patterns over time by the same customer reveals a lot about that customer – a customer may use his/her own name and address as a search term at some point in time and he/she also may search for information on medical conditions. When these different search queries are combined, a profile of that customer with a rich set of personal information can be created.). Even when customer data is not involved, other data released as part of the competition may contain sensitive corporate data that can be exploited by competing firms.

Enterprise 2.0

Many firms have begun to use mass collaboration technologies and processes internally. Some have begun to use the term Enterprise'2.0 to refer to a new set of collaborative processes and technologies adapted from the more popularlyPused Web 2.0 collaboration technologies. These technologies include instant messaging, blogs, and wikis.

At the outset, mass collaboration internal to the organization requires a change in mindset. It is hard to imagine the entire organization collaborating on projects in a self-organized manner. So how exactly can mass collaboration be achieved? We mentioned that the power law distribution is one of the underlying reasons that a large base of users can self-organize to peer-produce content. However, corporate divisions do not have a large base of employees. They are also governed by a different set of norms and practices. Corporate employees have accountability – their contributions would be judged more closely by their managers and peers. While peer producers can self-organize and do not have any internal deadlines to work with, corporate employees constantly juggle with multiple responsibilities and deadlines. Therefore it is not easy to assume that mass collaboration processes and technologies can be easily adapted inside corporate settings. Any implementation of Web 2.0 technologies and processes should justify the investments in time and money.
Some examples of mass collaboration tools inside corporations have begun to emerge. These early efforts show promising new ways through which new forms of collaboration models can be defined inside the organization.
• The'Geek'Squad division within Best Buy generates over $1 billion in revenues through customized computer services for individual consumers in the US. As the division grew from 60 to over 10,000 employees, Geek Squad members used online games such as Battlefield 2 to stay in touch. While playing games, these members exchanged valuable tips and tricks related to their work18. At the same time, a corporate-sponsored wiki system was very slow to take off within the organization. The success of the selfP-rganized network in Geek Squad has encouraged Best Buy to try new forms of bottomPup collaborations in its retail division through initiatives such as leadership forums that gather customer insights from frontline employees.
• DrKW Dresdner Kleinwort Wasserstein) is an investment bank based in London. The bank has experimented extensively with wikis and blogs in its internal processes19. While the initial use of wikis and blogs began in the IT division, it has been slowly expanded to include the bank’s main business divisions as well. The IT division uses wikis mainly to manage meeting minutes, document procedures and how-tos, and create presentations. The firm has found that it is important to create some initial content to guide users in the beginning and then let the employees selfP organize the content in their own way through an adaptive process. Over 2500 employees use the wikis and blogs regularly. Interestingly, to promote widespread use of blogs, the firm included a link to the employee blogs inside the Outlook contact information in the same way that phone and fax numbers are included.

There are also a number of collaboration sites that operate independently from the connected firms and yet are entirely devoted to the product. For example, www.hardforum.com operates a number of forums for computer components; www.howardforum.com operates a number of forums for cell phone users. The users on these sites have developed their own set of norms on how to communicate. A community norm on hardforum.com is for posters to list the products and components they currently use in their signatures. Users can also be harsh on any posters who “pose” expertise. Real expertise is highly valued – longtime posters often maintain their own blogs to keep a current list of tips and tricks for specific components or gadgets.

One of the challenges to running a customer-centric wiki is the lack of control on content. If a firm owns the site and begins to delete posts for a variety of reasons, it can backfire. At the same time, it is counterproductive to retain information and posts that are inflammatory or inaccurate. Managing this delicate balance can be time-consuming and expensive. This is one of the reasons that independent product forums have been more successful. In fact, some firms encourage their employees to participate in these forums to provide additional information.
Firms also need to design mechanisms to align incentives. Some firms have experimented with creation of micro-payment or credit systems to encourage users to post solutions or help other customers. The credits accumulated over time can be used towards buying new products. Lego a maker of interlocking plastic blocks) has been experimenting with customerP generated ideas for quite sometime. Lego has created a software tool called Mindstorms for users to create their own toy robots. Once a user builds a design for a robot, they can even post their programs online for other customers to use. Lego has extended this concept further to allow users to assemble custom kits of Lego blocks. In fact, other customers can also purchase the custom kit once the customer shares the design voluntarily on the site.
Mass collaboration is an exciting development. As with any new technology or platform, there is considerable amount of enthusiasm among users to produce, share, and consume content online. It is important to know that there is some amount of skepticism on the sustainability and value of mass collaboration. First, quality of available content is an issue of concern. Given the vast number of blogs that exist today on the WWW, it is natural that there are many blogs that are of dubious quality. Second, validity and accuracy of the content cannot always be verified or assumed. These two concerns should at least cause readers to critically review the content before accepting its validity or accuracy. Yet, these concerns have always been around even in print media. Additionally, the higher quality content is more likely to receive higher visibility through the “small-worlds” phenomenon. The better content is more likely to be cited and quoted often, and it is also likely to link to other content on the web – and show up first on search engine results.

ModuleSummary

As indicated early on in this course, business processes and strategies should be foremost in our thoughts when we consider technology solutions. The course modules were designed to emphasize the understanding of technology use in the context of business needs.

Despite the dot.com bust, technology innovation is still thriving. The developments in the Web 2.0 space are a testament to the vibrant innovative community that is redefining business models and social interactions. The unrelenting rise in computing, communication, and storage power continues to create opportunities for new ways of analyzing information and create opportunities to create new technology-enabled business models.
Technology also has a disruptive influence on markets and industries. Collaborative technologies are allowing firms to innovate on their sourcing models. These new sourcing models are not only enabling firms to execute their processes globally; they are also creating entirely new markets for their products and services. Consequently, IT is too important a topic to be ignored by managers.
New technologies and terminologies can often be confusing and daunting for non-technical managers who tend to delegate technology decisions to their IT departments. So the main objective of this course was to provide a business context for prominent technologies in the marketplace today. Once managers realize that they can approach technology solutions by first identifying and exploring the business imperatives, they can better communicate to the technical staff on the technology requirements. This way, both technology and business executives can collaborate more closely to create value for the firm and its customers.
