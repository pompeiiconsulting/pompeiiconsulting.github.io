Organizational Strategy

  • Organization's design and work processes
  • Optimize organizational design
  • Optimize management control systems
  • Organization structure is the way of designing an organization so decision rights are correctly allocated.
  • The structure of reporting relationships reflects the flow of communication and decision making in an organization.

Organizational Structures

Hierarchical Organizational Structure

  • Description: Bureaucratic with defined levels of management
  • Characteristics: Division of labor specialization, unity of command
  • Best Environment: Stable Certain
  • Basis of Structure: Primary function
  • Power Structure: Centralized
  • Key Supporting Technology: Mainframe, centralized data and processing

Flat Organizational Structure

  • Description: Decision-making pushed down to lowest level
  • Characteristics: Informal roles, planning and control; often small, young organizations
  • Best Environment: Unstable Uncertain
  • Basis of Structure: Primary function
  • Power Structure: Centralized
  • Key Supporting Technology: Personal computers

Matrix Organizational Structure

  • Description: Workers assigned to two or more supervisors
  • Characteristics: Dual reporting based on function/purpose
  • Best Environment: Unstable Uncertain
  • Basis of Structure: Functions and purpose
  • Power Structure: Distributed
  • Key Supporting Technology: Networks

Networked Organizational Structure

  • Description: Formal/informal communication networks that connect all
  • Characteristics: Known for flexibility and adaptability
  • Best Environment: Unstable Uncertain
  • Basis of Structure: Networks
  • Power Structure: Distributed
  • Key Supporting Technology: Intranets and Internet
  • Employees share their knowledge and experience,and participate in making key organizational decisions.
  • Information Systems are fundamental to process design, improve process efficiency, effectiveness, and flexibility.

Traditional: Subjective Observation

  • Supervision: Personal and informal. Manager is usually present or relies on others to ensure that employee is present and productive.
  • Evaluation: Focus is on process through direct observation. Manager sees how employee performed at work. Subjective (personal) factors are very important.
  • Compensation: Often individually-based.
  • Hiring: Personal with little reliance on computers. Often more reliance on clerical skills.

Modern: Objective Observation

  • Supervision: Electronic or assessed by deliverable. As long as the employee is producing value, he does not need formal supervisions.
  • Evaluation: Focus is on output by deliverable (e.g., produce a report by a certain date) or by target (e.g., meet a sales quota). As long as deliverables are produced and/or targets are achieved, the employee is meeting performance expectations adequately. Subjective factors may be less important and harder to gauge.
  • Compensation: Often team-based or contractually spelled out.
  • Hiring: Often electronic with recruiting websites and electronic testing. More information- based work that requires a higher level of IT skills.

Telecommuting

  • Started in 1970's
  • Gained popularity in late 1990's
  • reduces stress
  • geographic flexibility
  • higher productivity
  • tapping housebound individuals
  • difficult for managers to evaluate performance
  • workers must be extremely self disciplined
  • may end up working more hours
  • can be disconnected from corporate culture
  • offshoring enabled by same technology

Managerial Issues in Telecommuting

  • planning, business, and support tasks must be redesigned to support mobile and remote workers
  • training should be offered so all workers understand the new work environment
  • employees must be self starters
  • managers must fined new ways to evaluate and supervise employees without seeing them everyday

Virtual Teams

Geographically or organizationally dispersed coworkers assembled using information technology and telecommunications

  • same drivers as telecommuting
  • teams in different parts of the world can cooperate
    • get jobs done faster
    • around the clock
  • different time zones
  • security is harder to ensure
  • lose nuances of face to face conversation
  • trust may be slower to form
  • diversity of team members
    • language
    • culture
    • customs
